# 7-Question Structural Reasoning Chain

Every agent applies this framework to major themes before generating predictions.

## I. Actors & Structural Needs

Who are the actors and what does their structural position FORCE them to do?
Distinguish stated positions from structural imperatives.

## I-b. Deep Motivations (CRITICAL)

Before constraints analysis, identify the TRUE objective function. Check six forces:

1. **Religious/theological frameworks** — regime legitimacy tied to theology (e.g., Iran nuclear program)
2. **Civilizational identity & historical destiny** — "century of humiliation," imperial restoration (e.g., China-Taiwan, Russia-Ukraine)
3. **Collective trauma & generational memory** — hyperinflation memory (e.g., ECB hawkishness), "never again" doctrine (e.g., Israeli security)
4. **Honor, face & status hierarchies** — humiliation avoidance overriding economic logic (e.g., Saudi, North Korea)
5. **Ideological true belief** — leaders who genuinely believe, not cynically using ideology (e.g., Xi's Marxism, European climate commitment)
6. **Tribal/ethnic/kinship structures** — informal loyalty overriding formal institutions (e.g., Afghanistan, Iraq, Libya state-building failures)

## II. Constraints

What can't happen? Narrow infinite possibilities to 2-3 viable paths.
Include identity constraints from I-b.

## III. Irreversibilities

One-way doors that lock in outcomes regardless of future events.
Example: 2022 reserve freeze → dollar diversification is structurally locked in.

## IV. Equilibrium

Given true objective functions + constraints, where does the system settle?
Is current state stable or must it break?

## V. Consensus Error

Where is consensus wrong, and what structural reason explains the error?
Examples: applying economic model to geopolitical situation, ignoring irreversibilities.

## VI. Second/Third-Order Effects

Follow the cascade further than anyone else.
Does the policy produce the opposite of its intended effect at the third order?
